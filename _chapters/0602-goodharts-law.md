# Goodhart's Law

*When a measure becomes a target, it ceases to be a good measure.*

---

A hospital in England had a problem with waiting times. Patients were arriving at A&E and sitting for hours before being seen. The government noticed. A target was set: every patient must be seen within four hours of arrival.

The hospitals hit the target.

They did it by keeping patients in ambulances in the car park. The clock did not start until the patient crossed the threshold. So patients waited, sometimes in pain, sometimes deteriorating, while the hospital's four-hour record stayed clean.

The nurses and paramedics knew. They could see what was happening. They understood that the number being reported bore no resemblance to the experience of the patients they were caring for. But the metric was green. Leadership believed things were improving. And raising a concern that contradicted the official picture, in an environment where performance was being watched, felt like a risk most people were not willing to take.

So they absorbed it. The stress, the frustration, the cognitive dissonance of knowing the system was broken while being told it was working. That cost does not appear anywhere in the data. It accumulates quietly, in the people doing the work, until it shows up as burnout, disengagement, or absence.

The metric looked fine right up until the system did not.

---

## The Principle
**The moment a measure becomes the target, people start moving the number instead of changing the reality it was meant to reflect — and the measure stops telling you anything true.**

A KPI is a diagnostic signal. It tells you whether something is healthy, like a temperature gauge, or a blood pressure reading. The value of it lies in its accuracy. When it drifts outside the normal range, something needs attention. When it sits within range, you can proceed.

A target is a directive. It says: get this number to this point, and keep it there.

The moment you confuse the two, you lose both. People will move the number without changing the underlying reality, because moving the number is now the job. And the signal, the one thing the measure was supposed to give you, becomes useless, because it no longer reflects what is actually happening.

## Why It Is Inevitable

People want to be seen as doing well. Not because they are cynical, but because that is how organisations work. Performance is visible. Underlying reality often is not.

When a specific number is chosen to represent success, effort naturally flows toward that number. Not maliciously. Rationally. The organisation said this is how we are measuring progress, and people are responding accordingly.

There is also something more subtle at work. If every measure stays green, the system feels comfortable. There is no urgency, no hard conversation, no need for uncomfortable decisions. Green lights feel like good management. The problem is that a system where every light stays green is a system that has stopped learning. Continuous improvement depends on feedback, on things occasionally going wrong in ways you can see and act on. If the data is always positive, the signal has been lost, and with it, any real ability to make the system better.

## How It Shows Up

- Metrics that look healthy while the underlying system quietly deteriorates
- Frontline staff aware that reported performance does not match experienced reality, but unwilling to say so
- A proliferation of additional metrics added to compensate for ones being gamed, each creating its own distortion
- Short-term decisions that improve the number while damaging the capability behind it
- An organisation that mistakes the absence of red lights for evidence of health

## Why It Causes Damage

The damage is threefold.

The system stops improving. Effort goes into hitting the number rather than fixing what the number was supposed to represent. The hospital does not get faster. It gets better at managing when the clock starts.

Visibility disappears. A measure being gamed tells you nothing. It looks like information but it is not. Decisions get made on the basis of numbers that no longer reflect reality, and the gap between what leadership believes is happening and what is actually happening quietly widens. By the time the gap becomes visible, it is usually large.

And the people who know pay a hidden cost. Frontline workers who can see the disconnect between reported and actual performance are placed in an impossible position. Speaking up means contradicting a metric that leadership has invested in. Staying quiet means absorbing a form of institutional dishonesty day after day. That accumulates. It shows up as cynicism, stress, disengagement, and eventually in the numbers nobody thought to measure.

The trouble is reliably misdiagnosed. When the number looks wrong, the instinct is to blame the reporting — but better tools capturing the wrong data improve nothing. Or to blame the people, scrutinising their effort and commitment while never asking whether the thing being measured was the right thing at all. Or to call for more resource, when the real shortage is signal, not capacity — there is no honest feedback left to show where investment would actually help, because the data has been tuned for appearance rather than accuracy. In each case the measure gets defended and the wrong fix gets applied.

## How To Counter It
- Preserve the distinction between a KPI and a target deliberately — the first is a signal to be read, the second a directive to be hit, and confusing them destroys both.
- Give a KPI a healthy range rather than a fixed point. Drifting outside it is information to be understood, not a failure to be hidden — an organisation that conceals breaches has lost its only means of detecting real problems.
- Pair any measure of speed or quantity with a measure of quality. Track how fast and how well; track cost and what is being built. Single metrics get gamed; paired ones are far harder to game at once.
- Separate measurement from judgement. If hitting a number decides whether someone is seen as successful, the number will be hit one way or another.

## What Good Looks Like

Leaders use metrics to ask questions, not to deliver verdicts. Numbers that drift outside their normal range trigger curiosity, not panic. People surface problems in the data rather than conceal them, because falling outside a healthy range is understood as useful information, not evidence of failure.

The measure and the reality it represents stay connected, because no one has been given an incentive to pull them apart. And because the signal remains honest, the system can actually improve.

## A Reflective Question

What metric in your organisation is currently showing green, and what would you find if you looked at the reality behind it?
