# The First Shortcut Sets the Standard

*Early compromises become permanent norms.*

---

A new system is going live and the deadline is tight. The plan said every record would be checked before migration, but there is no time, so someone makes a call: skip the checks on the older records, migrate them as-is, clean them up later. It is a reasonable trade under pressure. The launch happens. Nothing visibly breaks.

"Later" never comes, because later there is always a next deadline. But something more lasting has happened. The next person to face a tight launch looks at how the last one was done and sees that skipping the checks was fine — it shipped, nobody was punished, the sky did not fall. So they skip them too, with slightly less hesitation. The one after that does not even register it as a shortcut. It is just how migrations are done here.

The original decision was meant to be an exception. It quietly became the rule, not because anyone decided it should, but because the first time set the reference point and every time after measured itself against that.

What started as "just this once, under pressure" is now the standard, and no one remembers it was ever supposed to be temporary.

---

## The Principle
**The first time a corner is cut without consequence, it stops being a corner cut and becomes the new baseline that everything afterward is judged against.**

People calibrate "normal" from what they observe, not from what the rules say. A shortcut that is taken once and survives sets a precedent: it demonstrates that this is permitted, tolerated, and safe. The next decision starts from there.

## Why It Is Inevitable
Behaviour is anchored to precedent, and the most recent example is the strongest anchor. When someone faces a decision, they do not reason it out from first principles; they look at what was done last time and adjust slightly. So the first instance of a shortcut becomes the reference point for the second, which becomes the reference for the third. The standard drifts in whatever direction the first exception pointed.

This is reinforced because the shortcut, by definition, was taken to relieve pressure — and the pressure that produced it rarely goes away. The conditions that justified it once tend to recur, and each time they do, the precedent is sitting there ready to be used, now with the added authority of "we did this before and it was fine."

And the absence of consequence is itself the lesson. A shortcut that caused visible harm would be a warning. A shortcut that caused no visible harm is an endorsement. Because consequences are often delayed or land elsewhere, the early instances almost always look successful, which is exactly what cements them.

## How It Shows Up
- "We did it that way last time" cited as sufficient justification.
- A documented standard that no longer matches actual practice, and everyone knows which one is real.
- Exceptions made under pressure that quietly become the default once the pressure passes.
- New people learning the shortcut as the method, unaware it was ever a compromise.
- Standards that only ever ratchet downward, never back up.

## Why It Causes Damage
The drift is one-directional and self-concealing. Each step down is small and justified by the step before it, so there is never a moment that looks like a fall in standards — only a series of small, defensible continuations. By the time the gap between the stated standard and the real one is obvious, it has been normal for so long that restoring the standard feels like an unreasonable new demand rather than a return.

And the bill arrives late. The skipped checks, the deferred cleanup, the tolerated compromise accumulate into a liability that surfaces long after the precedent was set — at which point it is blamed on whoever was holding it last, not on the first reasonable shortcut that set the whole pattern running.

## How To Counter It
- Treat the first instance as the decision it actually is — you are not handling one case, you are setting a standard. Decide accordingly.
- Make exceptions visibly temporary: name an expiry, a condition, an owner for the "later," so the shortcut cannot silently become the rule.
- When invoking "we did it that way last time," ask whether last time was a considered standard or an unexamined precedent.
- Watch for standards that only ever loosen. A norm that never tightens is drifting, not stable.
- Restore lapsed standards deliberately and early, while the gap is still small enough to close without a fight.

## What Good Looks Like
Places where a one-off compromise is recorded as a one-off — with a reason, a boundary, and a date — so it cannot quietly install itself as the baseline. Where the stated standard and the practised standard are the same thing, and divergence between them is treated as a problem to fix rather than a reality to accept. Where the first person to cut a corner knows they are writing the rule for everyone who comes after.

Exceptions still get made — pressure is real — but they stay exceptions, because someone made sure they could not become the standard by default.

## A Reflective Question
Think of something your team now does "the quick way" as standard. Can you remember the first time it was done that way — and was that first time ever meant to be permanent?
