# When Metrics Replace Judgment

**Category:** Incentives, metrics, and consequences
*A number meant to inform a decision quietly becomes the decision.*

---

A clinic sets a target: ninety-five per cent of patients seen within four hours. It is a sensible target. It was chosen because long waits were causing real harm, and someone wanted a way to know whether things were getting better. For a while it works. People can see the number, the number gets better, and the waits genuinely come down.

Then a patient arrives who is complicated. She does not fit the four-hour shape. She needs a test that takes longer than that, and a conversation that takes longer still, and the honest clinical answer is that she should wait — properly, safely, for the right thing. But the clock is running, and the clock is what the manager sees, and the manager has a meeting on Thursday where the number will be read aloud. So she is moved. Admitted to a ward she does not need, or discharged with a follow-up that will quietly bring her back, or relabelled so the clock resets. The four-hour figure stays green. The judgment that the target existed to protect — is this person being looked after properly — has been switched off, because there is now a greener, faster, more visible thing to optimise instead.

Nobody decided to stop using their judgment. That is the part worth sitting with. There was no meeting where someone proposed that clinical sense be replaced by a stopwatch. The number was only ever meant to be a hint, a rough proxy for "are we caring for people in good time." But a proxy that is measured, reported, and rewarded does not stay a hint. It hardens. It stops standing in for the thing and starts standing in front of it.

You can watch the same thing happen anywhere a number gets a seat at the table. A sales team measured on calls made starts making short, useless calls and avoiding the long, awkward, deal-winning ones. A team of developers measured on tickets closed splits one real problem into six tidy tickets and leaves the messy, important bug — the one that resists being a ticket — untouched. A school measured on a single grade boundary pours its energy into the handful of pupils sitting just below the line and lets the rest, the ones it cannot move across a threshold this year, fend for themselves. In every case the people involved are competent and well-meaning. In every case they have quietly stopped asking "what is the right thing to do here?" and started asking "what moves the number?" — and those two questions, which were supposed to give the same answer, have come apart.

---

## The Principle
**A metric is a compressed proxy for something we actually care about but cannot easily see. Once the proxy is measured and rewarded, people optimise the proxy directly — and the judgment it was meant to summarise gets crowded out, because the number is now easier to satisfy than the reality it stood for.**

We measure things because the thing that matters is hard to observe. We cannot see "good care," "good engineering," "a healthy team," or "a satisfied customer" directly, so we find a number that tends to move with them and watch that instead. The trouble is that the number and the thing are not the same. They merely correlate, under the conditions that held when we chose the number. Optimise the number hard enough and you stretch that correlation until it snaps — and by then the number is the goal and the original concern has no one watching it.

## Why It Is Inevitable
A number is legible and judgment is not. You can put a metric on a dashboard, compare it across teams, track it over time, and hold someone to it in a one-line email. You cannot do any of that with "she used her judgment and it was sound." Judgment is slow to explain, hard to audit, and impossible to compare cleanly between two people. So the moment an organisation needs to coordinate at scale — to manage people it cannot personally watch — it reaches for numbers, because numbers are the only thing that travels.

That reach is reasonable. The failure comes next, and it comes from a quiet substitution that nobody authorises. A metric starts life as *evidence for* a judgment: the waiting time is one input among several into the question of whether care is good. But evidence that is reported every week, owned by a named person, and tied to their standing slowly gets promoted. It becomes the question. "Is care good?" is replaced by "is the number green?" — not by decision, but by attrition, because the second question can be answered and the first cannot.

And there is a deeper reason it sticks: a metric absolves you of judgment, and judgment is exposing. If I hit the target and something still goes wrong, I followed the rule and the rule was the problem. If I overrode the target on a hunch and something goes wrong, the failure is mine, personally, and I will be asked why I thought I knew better than the number. Given that asymmetry, deferring to the metric is not laziness. It is self-protection. People do not abandon judgment because they have stopped caring. They abandon it because the number is safer to be wrong with.

There is also momentum behind it. A number, once it is on a dashboard, acquires a constituency. Someone built the report. Someone presents it. Someone's bonus is pinned to it, and someone's last reorganisation was justified by it. The metric stops being a question the organisation is asking and becomes a fact the organisation defends, and the energy that should go into checking whether it still measures anything goes instead into protecting it from doubt. The longer a proxy survives, the harder it is to question, precisely because more and more of the place has been built on the assumption that it is true.

## How It Shows Up
- Decisions get justified by citing the metric rather than by reasoning about the situation: "we did it because it kept us inside the target."
- People who raise a sensible exception are answered with "yes, but the number," as though that settles it.
- The metric is hit and the thing it was supposed to indicate is visibly worse, and nobody treats that as a contradiction.
- Experienced people stop arguing for what they think is right and start arguing about how the number is calculated.
- "We're not allowed to use our judgment any more" — said with resignation, by the very people hired for theirs.
- Effort flows toward whatever the metric can see, and quietly drains away from everything it cannot.

## Why It Causes Damage
The first damage is that the real goal goes unguarded. Once everyone is optimising the proxy, the actual thing the proxy stood for has no defender — it has become, like the system-wide outcome that no single team owns, a concern that belongs to no one. It degrades silently, because the only instrument anyone is watching is the one that says everything is fine.

The second damage is subtler and worse: the metric trains people out of the very faculty the organisation hired them for. Judgment is a muscle. It develops through being used — through making the call, watching what happens, and adjusting. A regime that punishes overriding the number and rewards obeying it gives that muscle nothing to do, and it wastes. After a few years you have a workforce of capable people who have learned that thinking for themselves is a liability, and who will now reliably do the measured wrong thing in preference to the unmeasured right one. You did not just misjudge one patient. You built an institution that cannot tell when its own numbers have stopped pointing at anything.

And the failure hides itself. Because the metric stays green, there is no alarm — the same dangerous quiet as a system where every part reports success while the whole gets worse. The dashboard is, by construction, the last place the problem will appear, because the problem *is* that the dashboard has come unhooked from reality. By the time the gap surfaces, it surfaces as a scandal: a run of bad outcomes that the numbers swore could not be happening.

## How To Counter It
- Hold the metric and the goal apart, out loud and repeatedly. Name what the number is a proxy *for*, and treat any divergence between them as information about the metric, not a nuisance to be managed away.
- Make overriding the number safe — and expected. If using judgment against the metric is always a personal risk and never an institutional one, you have priced judgment out. Ask people to log the override and the reasoning, and judge the reasoning, not just the outcome.
- Periodically ask the heretical question: if we maximised this metric ruthlessly, would we be doing our actual job better or worse? If the honest answer is "worse," the metric has decoupled and needs retiring or rebalancing.
- Watch for the proxy hardening into the target. The warning sign is when "is the number good" has quietly replaced "is the work good" in how people talk — when the metric is cited as a reason rather than offered as evidence.
- Keep more than one number, and keep them in tension. A single metric is an invitation to optimise it to death; a small set that pulls in different directions forces a judgment call back into the room, which is exactly where you wanted it.
- Protect the people who still argue with the dashboard. They are not being difficult. They are doing the part of the job a number cannot do, and they are the early-warning system for the day the proxy stops being true.

## What Good Looks Like
Organisations that treat metrics as instruments, not verdicts — useful, trusted within their range, and never mistaken for the thing they measure. Where a green number that sits next to a bad outcome triggers a question about the number, not a celebration. Where someone is allowed to say "the figure says we're fine and we are not fine," and be taken seriously rather than told to mind the figure. Where the experienced are still expected to think, and the act of thinking — of overriding the proxy when the proxy is wrong — is recorded, examined, and honoured rather than quietly held against them.

The numbers still matter. You cannot run anything at scale without them. But they are kept in their place: a summary of judgment, offered to it, never a replacement for it.

## A Reflective Question
Look at the number you are most often asked to deliver. If you set out to make it perfect tomorrow, with no other consideration, would the thing it was invented to protect get better — or would you simply have learned to satisfy the gauge while the work it stands for quietly slips out from under it?
