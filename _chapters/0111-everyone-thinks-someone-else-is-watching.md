# Everyone Thinks Someone Else Is Watching

**Category:** Team dynamics
*Accountability weakens when oversight is assumed.*

---

A report goes out to a client with a wrong figure in it. Not a small one. It is caught, eventually, by the client, which is the worst way to catch it.

In the post-mortem, everyone is genuinely baffled, because the report passed through five people before it left. The analyst who built it assumed the manager would check the numbers, since that was the point of the review step. The manager assumed the analyst had checked them and that the review was for tone and framing. The two reviewers assumed that if the numbers were wrong, surely one of the others would have flagged it by now — look how many eyes were on this. The person who hit send assumed it had been signed off, because why else would it be in their outbox.

Five competent people looked at the report. Each one, reasonably, relied on the others. The more people in the chain, the more each individual felt covered, and the less anyone actually checked. A document with one reviewer would have been checked once. This one, with five, was checked by no one.

Nobody was careless. The carelessness was structural. It lived in the gaps between five people who all assumed the gap was someone else's.

---

## The Principle
**When responsibility is shared without being assigned, each person assumes someone else has it — and the more people involved, the safer that assumption feels and the less likely it is to be true.**

Diffusion of responsibility is not laziness. It is a rational inference: the more eyes on a problem, the more reasonable it becomes to assume one of them is the watching eye. Everyone makes the same inference, so everyone relaxes by the same amount.

## Why It Is Inevitable
Adding people to a task feels like adding safety. Intuitively, more reviewers means more chances to catch the error. But each reviewer's diligence is not fixed — it falls as the group grows, because the felt personal stake falls. If you are the only checker, the error is unambiguously yours. If you are one of five, you can tell yourself a true story in which it was someone else's job.

This is sharpened by ambiguity about who owns what. "The team reviews it" assigns the duty to a collective, and a collective cannot check anything — only a named person can. When the role is "everyone," the role is effectively no one, because no individual can be held to a duty that was never theirs in particular.

And the assumption is invisible. Nobody announces "I am relying on the others." They simply do, quietly, and so does everyone else, and the absence of checking looks exactly like the presence of it right up until something gets through.

## How It Shows Up
- "I assumed someone else had checked that."
- Big, well-attended reviews that nonetheless miss obvious things.
- Adding reviewers to a process that keeps producing errors — and the errors continuing.
- Tasks owned by "the team" rather than by a person.
- After a failure, a chain of people who each reasonably thought it was covered.
- The bystander effect: a problem visible to many, acted on by none.

## Why It Causes Damage
The errors that slip through diffused responsibility are often the obvious ones, which makes them especially expensive — they are the kind everyone assumes could not possibly have been missed, so trust is built on the assumption that they were caught. The damage is not just the error; it is the false confidence that a crowd of reviewers creates.

It also scales the wrong way. The instinctive fix for a missed check is to add another checker, which lowers everyone's individual sense of ownership further and makes the next miss more likely. The process grows heavier and less reliable at the same time, and the failure gets blamed on careless individuals when the real culprit was the structure that made carelessness rational.

## How To Counter It
- Assign responsibility to a named person, not a group. "Who owns this?" should always have a single answer.
- Give each reviewer a distinct, non-overlapping job — one checks figures, one checks framing — so no two people can both assume the other had it.
- Resist the urge to fix missed checks by adding checkers. Often the fix is fewer people with clearer ownership, not more.
- Make the assumption explicit: ask people what they are relying on others to do, and watch the gaps appear.
- Treat "the team will handle it" as an unassigned task until proven otherwise.

## What Good Looks Like
Work where every critical step has exactly one owner who knows the step is theirs and cannot quietly assume it away. Where adding people adds genuine, differentiated scrutiny rather than diluting the scrutiny that already exists. Where reviews are designed so that no two reviewers are doing the same job and hoping the other one really did it.

Many eyes can be a strength — but only when each pair is looking at something different and knows it.

## A Reflective Question
Think of a task that several people are "responsible" for. If it failed tomorrow, how many of them could honestly say it was their job to catch it — and would any two of them name the same person?
