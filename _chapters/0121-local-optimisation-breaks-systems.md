# Local Optimisation Breaks Systems

**Category:** Organisations and systems
*Improving one part of a system can damage the whole.*

---

A warehouse manager is measured on one thing: how fast goods leave the loading dock. She is good at her job, so she gets faster. She batches orders to cut handling time, ships the easy lines first, and pushes anything awkward — part-picks, fragile items, custom packing — to the back of the queue. Her numbers are the best in the region. She is held up as an example.

Two doors down, the returns team is drowning. The awkward orders that kept getting deferred now arrive late, in a rush, packed badly to make up time. Customers complain. Returns climb. The returns team's numbers are the worst in the region, and nobody can work out why, because on paper the warehouse is performing brilliantly.

Each team is doing exactly what it was asked to do. The warehouse optimises for dispatch speed. Returns optimises for processing returns. Neither is measured on the thing that actually matters — whether the customer got the right thing, intact, on time. That number belongs to no one, so no one defends it.

The warehouse manager's improvement was real. It just wasn't free. It was paid for, in full, by a team she never sees, against a number nobody owns.

---

## The Principle
**Optimising a part of a system, in isolation, almost always pushes cost onto another part — and the local win is visible while the system-wide loss is not.**

A system is a set of parts that trade with each other. Tighten one part and the slack does not vanish; it moves. When the part is measured and the whole is not, the move looks like pure gain.

## Why It Is Inevitable
People are measured on the part they control, not the system they sit inside. That is how accountability is built — give someone a clear number and hold them to it. But a clear number for the part is an incentive to improve the part at the expense of whatever lies outside its boundary.

The costs of local optimisation are externalities. They land on someone else's number, in someone else's department, often at a later time. The person optimising rarely sees them and is never charged for them. So the rational move — improve your own metric, ignore the spillover — is also the locally responsible one. Nobody is being selfish. Everybody is being measured.

And the whole is harder to see than the parts. Parts have owners, dashboards, and meetings. The system between them usually has none of those, so its degradation goes unwatched while each part proudly improves.

## How It Shows Up
- One team's metrics improve sharply while a neighbouring team's quietly worsen.
- "That's not our number" — costs land on a team with no power to fix the cause.
- Hand-offs between departments are where things consistently break.
- A locally praised efficiency turns out to have created work elsewhere.
- Everyone is hitting their targets and the customer is still unhappy.

## Why It Causes Damage
The system gets worse while every component reports success, which is the most dangerous failure mode there is — because there is no alarm. Each owner can show that they did their job. The damage accumulates in the gaps between them, where no metric looks, until it surfaces as a problem nobody seems responsible for.

Worse, the incentive teaches itself. Once people see that local optimisation is rewarded and its spillover is unpunished, they optimise harder, and they stop volunteering information that might slow them down. The hand-offs degrade further. The system fragments into a set of locally excellent parts that, together, serve the customer badly.

## How To Counter It
- Measure at the level of the outcome that matters, not only at the level of the part — own the end-to-end number, and give it to someone with the authority to defend it.
- Make spillover visible: when a team improves, ask explicitly where the displaced cost went.
- Pay attention to the hand-offs. The boundaries between teams are where systems break and where no one is measured.
- Reward people for helping a neighbouring team hit its number, not only their own.
- Be suspicious of a local improvement that has no apparent cost. The cost is almost always real and simply landing somewhere you are not looking.

## What Good Looks Like
Systems where someone owns the whole journey, not just a leg of it, and where local targets are set in service of the system outcome rather than against it. Where a team that improves its own number is asked, as a matter of routine, what that did to everyone downstream. Where the hand-offs are treated as part of someone's job rather than as the no-man's-land between jobs.

The parts still improve — but they improve in a direction that adds up.

## A Reflective Question
Look at the metric you are judged on. If you maximised it ruthlessly tomorrow, whose number would quietly get worse — and does anyone own the gap between you?
